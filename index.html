  1. Paper on Mental Models

    Kieras, David E, and Susan Bovair. "The Role of a Mental Model in Learning to Operate a
    Device." Cognitive science 8.3 (1984): 255-73.

    This is a really interesting article about how and why we are able to remember how to perform tasks on both complicated software and easy software. This helps us understand the role of mental models in User experience design, and why natural mapping matters so much.

  2. Paper on Organic Architecture

    Shin, B. Y. "A Study on the Organic Thought in the Works of Le Corbusier, Venturi, and Lynn."
    Journal of Asian Architecture and Building Engineering 8.2 (2009): 315-22.

    Similar to Jane Jacobs “Death and Life of American Cities” this paper echoes an important theme for my paper, that there is a relationship between how something is designed, and who it is designed for. And the genius of Le Corbusier, and Venturi was their insistence that organism and machine should work in harmony, and sustain themselves.

  3. The Book that inspired this project

    Venturi, Robert, et al. Learning from Las Vegas: the forgotten symbolism of architectural form. MIT press, 1977.

    This is the book that inspired this thesis, it is a dense review of 1950s architectural norms and practices. Not everything in the book is relevant to my thesis, but the areas that overlap with software design, design thinking, and agile are really useful. Venturi and Scott brown explore what makes a city useful, and laid out the frameworks for modern Design Thinking.

  4. User Addapting Interface Design

    Zhao, Wei et al. "A Method of Designing Smartphone Interface Based on the Extended User's
    Mental Model." Seventh International Conference on Electronics and Information Engineering, vol. 10322, International Society for Optics and Photonics, 2017, p. 103222O.

    This paper lays out a theoretical framework for creating layouts that respond and improve the more a user uses them (think the facebook timeline), they also explore why this would be useful. I disagree with their findings, but their framework is actually really useful.

  5. Brhel, M. et al. "Exploring Principles of User-Centered Agile Software Development: A Literature
    Review." Information and Software Technology, vol. 61, 2015, pp. 163-181, doi:10.1016/j.infsof.2015.01.004.

The History

Le Corbusier

Many artists and architects revere Le Corbusier. Personally, I love his works for their artistic and skillful values, but it is important to view his works critically as well because they represent one of the greatest faux pas designers make today. On the one hand, he was a master designer, his buildings and urban planning are arguably some of the most beautiful ever to have been to designed in the 20th century. He was infatuated with the idea of designing buildings that didn't just look good, but worked well with the environment that they were built in.  This architect, with no formal education, came up with many concepts that we still reference Today in user experience design. This includes the concepts of modularity in design and organic design.  While talking about the machine for living, He even wrote about design that would welcome advancing technology by working symbiotically with machines and people. In design thinking talk, it was clear that he was a champion for Designing for the user. all of this, however, is in sharp contrast with the fact that he never talked to the user, But instead drew from his own experience and intuition.

Even in the age of IDEO style design thinking, UX designers and Urban Planners often follow the model of Le Corbusier. We make many design decisions based on experience, and intuition rather than working in conjunction with users and validating our intuition with user testing.  

Le Corbusier may have not started the modern movement, so you can hardly blame him for many of the foibles of modernists, as Venturi and Scott Brown (Venturi et al. 1977), however, Le Corbusier was probably the single most influential modernist architect and fathered the infatuation with concrete in the Brutalist movement that influences a large majority of post-war construction in Europe.

Why Le Corbusier?

“He forced people to think what it will be like to live in an environment that is predetermined, ultimately he succeeded in actually building such kind of an environment” (Tungare )

Modernists made a lot of claims, many of which are very convincing. But these claims were dogmatic and based solely on observation. Over the years several Modernist Manifestos have been written, by both Le Corbusier and his followers. (Corbusier 1987) Many buildings were built in nonmodern settings, some that were successful, some that were not. *insert Boston City Hall example from JJ*.  But modernist constantly strived for the ideal utopia. This ideal took on several names,  such as Ebenezer Howard's 'Garden City', Frank Lloyd Wright's 'Broadacre City', and Le Corbusier's 'Radiant City'. All three of these utopias were a reaction to the unplanned and organic developed, nineteenth-century European-style city. To their credit, they strove to stop cities from focusing on short term solutions, and instead favor of a comprehensive one. (Fishman 1982) However, rare is the case where you can see a modernist city built from the ground up and can test the veracity of the Modernisms claims.

Relevance to UXD

Similar to software design, critiques cannot be made, if user testing has not been done. In UXD that is unexpectable, in modernism, that was conveniently not a part of their doctrine. Later in this paper, we will examine Chandigarh, Le Corbusier’s master-planned city in Punjab, India. Chandigarh is a case where we can actually examine the merits of a Modern designer’s approach to design. Because Le Corbusier was the only Modernist to succeed in building a complete modern Utopia, we can compare the teachings of Modernism and Master Planning, verse the organic city approach of User Experience design.

Urban renewal, and post-war reconstruction

Modernist had been looking for an excuse to rebuild Europe for decades, and sadly many Modernist welcomed the bombings of Europe during World War 2 as an opportunity to put modernism into practice.

Assumptions

Modernists and Brutalists alike believed that Victorian or Baroque details were superfluous and such buildings needed to be replaced. They were very dogmatic in their design philosophy, and were famously wrong. Projects like the Boston City Hall and the public housing projects in New York City were built to bring the poor closer to the rich, create common spaces for public discourse and believed in imposing rigid rules to their designs. In most cases, these designs failed to live up to the expectations of their architects. Many became havens for crime and eyesores for their city. (Jacobs 1961)

‘Door frames, windows, bricks, roof tiles, even screws should conform to uniform prescriptions. The new standards should be legislated by the League of Nations, and a universal technical language should be compulsorily taught throughout the world, Le Corbusier's followers urged in a 1928 modernist manifesto. Le Corbusier insisted that al1 measurements, al1 ventilating standards, all Lighting, ail equipment and appliances, and al1 domestic aesthetics should be the same for al1 latitudes and al1 needs. "We must find and apply new methods. .. lending themselves naturally to standardization, industrialization, tailorization." he wrote in 1929. 'O He believed that by living with the standardized norms of construction, a city environment could be made much disciplined.” (Tungare )

Chandigarh: Brutalism in modern context

                                

When you design for the user without designing with the user, users will either reject your system or use your system in a creative way to meet their needs. This applies to architecture, urban design, user experience design, and software.

Chandigarh was meant to be Le Corbusier's crowning accomplishment, and by all accounts it was. However, despite the city taking years of careful planning and construction, the city's government buildings, parliamentary hall, wide roads, and even stop lights, are used completely differently than the master architect originally intended them to be.

Most of India uses roundabouts, they are more similar to Europe than the US in this way. Whereas the majority of the US has deep easements, and buildings offset off the road, (so as to accommodate future growth) most of India looks nothing like this. If there are sidewalks, they are skinny, and buildings butt up against the road. Most cities in India were designed in ancient times (if designed at all) and have narrow, spaghetti-like streets. American Cities have sections of the city zoned for uses, which is ideal for car use, and terrible for walking. India grew before the car was around, and cities being walkable was essential to the economy. Despite being the capital of Punjab, India, Chandigarh city plan looked like it was designed for Indiana.

Chandigarh is proud of its wide roads or sectors organized by use. One of his most famous contribution to Urban Planning was that of strict separation of uses and societal functions. Workplaces would be separated from residences, shopping, entertainment, and government. Lending to super large park sectors, shopping sectors, government sectors and so on. And were possible, larger work sectors would be further subdivided into even smaller ones for office buildings compared to factories. This became the standard for the west, up until the present day.

(Tungare ) However, Chandigarh, with its rigidly organized sections, is probably the worst metropolitan area in India to be a pedestrian in.

Le Corbusier was very strict about how cities were organized and was obsessive about controlling every variable. He often used the phrase “The Plan is a Dictator” (which he always capitalized in his writing” (Tungare )

Chandigarh’s perfectly perpendicular grid affords for fewer roundabouts and more stop lights. However, the citizens of Chandigarh largely ignore them, and in some cases have you even created makeshift roundabouts in the center of very wide intersections.

Learning From Las Vegas

Intro to the book and Authors

While modernist were on circuits preaching urban renewal through the practice of destroying the old decadent building, and ideas like that of the Corbusier’s Radiant City, three architects from the University of Pennsylvania co-authored a book that would change the way we think about architecture, design, and prototyping. (Trufelman 2018) The book, Learning from Las Vegas (Venturi et al. 1977) centered around the unique design language and urban groath of that strange city that had popped up in the American West. In the late 60’s that strange city, Las Vegas, seemed to defy the assumptions that many Modernist had previously made, and offered a fascinating case study about what people really wanted in a city.

This city seemed to suggest that people didn’t behave the way that modernists assumed they did. Form could follow something other than function, it could also act as a symbol in a whole, such as the famous Duck and the Decorated Shed example. (Venturi et al. 1977) The crowds in Las Vegas also seemed to suggest that people didn’t want the minimalist pallet, features and open layouts of modernism.  And when cities grew organically, without the limitation of modern zoning ideals, such as separating uses into sectors, cities were easy to navigate, vibrant, and most interestingly, constantly changed.

Their response to modernism and the birth of postmodernism

At the University of Pennsylvania, the Furness Library is a decadent Victorian building, currently used by the School of Design. It was completed in the year 1890, and is now a designated National Historic Landmark. However, in the late 1950s, architects were debating whether it should be torn down, and replaced with something sleek, modern and concrete. Knocking down the old to make way for the new at any cost, was a battle hymn for the post-war modernist. Denise Scott Brown, and Robert Venturi, both faculty members at Penn,  were actively opposed to the demolition proposal (Trufelman 2018). And though an unpopular opinion at the time, appreciated the opulence and detail these Victorian buildings. Their firm would be asked to lead the restoration in the early 90s (Venturi, Scott Brown and Associates 1991)  This fight over the Library lead to Denise Scott Brown sharing one of her architectural fascinations with Robert Venturi, the curious city of Las Vegas. Eventually co-writing of Learning From Las Vegas, which is a lot of ways was a response to Modernism and Brutalism.

Why this matters to software design                

What makes Las Vegas special, is that Las Vegas is one of the only modern cities in the world to be designed in conjunctions with the people who use it, it wasn't designed just to be used, it was designed to be liked, and loved, to the point of addiction. The people who designed the city were very careful about even the most minor details and tested with real people often (Schüll 2012). That makes Vegas interesting to anyone who purports to be a User Centered Designer. And Vegas is an interesting case study for software developers because it embodies some of the principles we learn in Design Thinking and Agile, the two most common frameworks for thinking about software development right now. Las Vegas teaches to stop telling users how to use our product, and start designing opportunities for users to design with you.

Janes Jacobs

  1. Her response to Robert Moses

In 1941 a project by Robert Moses was approved that would connect New Jersey with Queens and Brooklyn by way of a superhighway that essentially cut lower Manhatten in half.  If you have ever tried to drive from Long Island to Jersey, then you know that this superhighway was never built. In fact, it was canceled in 1960 because of the public outrage. Why didn’t it happen? Well, Robert Moses chose the wrong neighborhood to build his superhighway in, SoHo, the lower Manhatten neighborhood of the famous columnist Jane Jacobs.

  1. Her principles of organic urban design

In the book that basically tanked this project, and saved SoHo, Jacobs describes the problems with the Modernist obsession of urban renewal and introduces the idea of the organic city. A city that is designed and built by the people who live there.

Design thinking Emerges

First in architecture: Peter Rowe

IDEO: Simplifies, and adapts the practice for businesses.

Ideo is widely credited for creating, defining and promoting our modern concept of Design Thinking. The brothers David and Tom Kelly championed the practices of ideation, rapid prototyping, using users and stakeholders as co-collaborators (as opposed to subjects) and iterating by failing fast and often. Through their company, they have evangelized this framework to be used as a “good idea generator,” useful in any industry where complex problems exist, and post-it notes are available. This framework, however, has probably been embraced the most in User Experience Design.

Architecture was only responsible for influencing a fraction of the technique we know today as design thinking. Although the phrase design thinking has architect Peter Rowe to thank, (Rowe and Raymond Garbe Professor of A...) design thinking also draw methods from the fields of Anthropology, Industrial Design (specifically Buckminster Fuller, and Don Norman), organization phycology, participatory design, and Ethnography. Ideo is important, because they took this very diverse and complicated set of frameworks, and organized it into something you could teach in 45 minutes at a business meeting.

Design thinking has changed and evolved a lot since the days of Robert Moses, Jane Jacobs and Peter Rowe. Despite urbanism inspiring much of the practices we use in design thinking, there is a lot that urbanist can change in order to make cities better, and improve the lives of those that live there.

How urban planners ideate and prototype in 2019

In the context of urban planning, up until now, we have been talking about how architects have imagined cities should be designed from the ground up. In reality, however, the vast majority of developments have to work within the constraints of what already exists. In the course of human history, very few cities have been built from a clean slate, this includes Chandigarh and Brasilia. Urban planners in 2019 need to consider a variety of factors that exist within the neighborhoods and environment within which they are designing.

Urban planning does not happen within the control of one person either there are several job titles that we can break urban planning down into.

Because City development it's so depending upon economic and governmental decision-making we will start from the top and work our way down.

 In many states, the Bureau of Economic Analysis will release an Economic Report to the Governor outlining the demand for housing, employment and the projected growth from within a state. Zoning offices within cities rely heavily on directions from the state when deciding how to zone for use.

In Utah for example, this economic report shows there is a shortage of about 75,000 homes. Meaning that there will be about 75,000 more homebuyers on the market than there are homes, driving up the demand on housing. Cities, though their zoning offices, can then plan for the higher demand in homes, by adjusting their master plan (usually updated every 10 years) to include more residential housing.

If you are a landowner, interested in developing your land, what you can build depends on this master plan, and what use this land has been zoned for. If your land was zoned for commecial use, and there was economic incentive to build commercial real estate, you would hire a civil engineer to work with the city to develop planning documents to show your proposed use.

If that plan meets zoning requirements, they will issues conditions of approval that state what the instructor needs to be approved in order to facilitate the project. If you want to develop your land for something other than what zoning has approved, you would have to hire civil engineeers to do a genral plan adjustment or land swap. THough this is hard and costly to do.

Throughout this whole process, economic factors, more than design factors drive growth. So to answer the question “how do Urban planners ideate and prototype in 2019” the answer is pretty simple, they don’t.

The position within this network that is given the most descretion in what will be built, is the local zoning office, they have more power over will be built than even the state.  The urban planners within these offices reference zoning ordinances passed by their city councils, and they reference libraries that help them calcutlate traffic congestion, or the amenities that they can support. They then prototype in AutoCAD, which is simply a Computer Aided Drafting software, that can run basic calculations.

What is missing is a holistic way of prototyping different configurations and development techniques.
